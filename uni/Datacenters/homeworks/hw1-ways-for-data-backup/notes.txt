# Slide 1 - Backups? What? (Basics)

Let's start with the basics, what even is a backup and why would you want to do it.

A backup is a copy of some data that is stored somewhere else other than the system that its being used by.
This data is used to restore the functionality of the said system in the case of some system failure or data corruption,
or an engineer accidentally nuking the production database.

Nobody is safe from any of those events, so without backups data loss is not a question of 'if', but 'when'.

Though, it doesn't always make sense to back up everything, typically only the critical data is backed up,
without any caches or runtime data.

-------------------------------------------------------------
# Slide 2 - How?

So, how do you backup?

The strategy largely depends on the type of data you are backing up, how important it is,
how frequently it's updated, cost constraints and a lot of other factors.

As a general advice you should follow the 3-2-1 rule. Keep at least 3 copies of data,
2 of them should be on different mediums (2 different models of hard drives of hdd and an ssd) and 1 copy should be off-site

Having copies on different mediums protects you from failure of 2 mediums at the same time (they wear out at the same time of get damaged the same way)
Having a copy off-site protects you from natural disasters or any other kinds of disasters happening to the space where your backups are stored
And having a third copy protects you from loosing the other 2, it's your last resort

There are different ways to back up the data as well
You could do periodic backups where you run the backup routine every N time units
The routine way then create a full, incremental or a differential backup, we will talk about them more later

Another way is to continuously replicate the data onto another storage device keeping only the latest version backed up

# Slide 3 - Backup strategy. Periodic backups

This is the most used and quite simple strategy.

Before implementing this strategy, you need to define your priorities
- How often and how do you want to back up? (backup type and frequency)
- How long you want to keep the backups for? (retention)
- Do you want to optimise for backup time, storage space, cpu usage of network bandwidth?

--
Backup frequency depends on the volume of data written and how fresh your latest backup needs to be
(how costly is the recovery), regulatory compliance and some other minor factors

Retention is typically set by some regulations, by storage constraints or common sense.

A good approach is to have a daily, weekly and monthly backups with different retention intervals for each

In some cases, like in financial or health sectors, some data may need to be backed up more frequently, e.g: hourly or even every few minutes.
Because it's very critical

And in other cases like in document management systems, where the data is mostly static, weekly backups may be enough

Depending on your priorities and constraints, you should choose a backup type or several
There are three types: full, differential and incremental. Each one good and bad in their own ways

///
Full backup is the easiest of them all, this is just a full copy, like you copying a file and naming it

- Pros:
= Complete copy is available with everything in it, the backup is portable
= Time to restore is minimal if you are not constrained by transfer speed

- Cons:
= Every backup is as large as your entire dataset
= Slow to perform since you need to read the entire dataset

///
Next one is differential backup, in this type we don't copy the whole dataset,
but only the difference since the last full backup. So this is used between full backups
to save space and time.

- Pros:
= Less space required since you don't store complete copies of the dataset. Especially
if your data doesn't change frequently
= It is faster to perform as well, since you don't need to copy everything, just compare
modification dates and checksums

- Cons:
= Slightly longer restore times, but not by much, since now you have to reconstruct your
dataset from the latest full backup and the desired incremental one.
= The backup is less portable, you need 2 backups if you want to restore to a non-full backed-up point


///
And the third type is incremental. In this type we don't store differences from the last FULL backup,
but to the previous backup, effectively recording just the delta.
This backup works more like version control (git).

- Pros:
= The least amount of space is used since there is no duplicated data between backups expect for full
backup checkpoints.
= The fastest to perform as well. The organization can therefore do them more often without requiring
beefy systems or a lot of storage

- Cons:
= Even longer restore times than differential backups since you have to combine ALL
the incremental parts to restore. This can cost a fair amount of cpu time as well
= The backup is not portable at all since you have to carry all the previous
incremental backups up until the restore point.
= The backup is more fragile. If any backup in the incremental chain gets corrupted or deleted, you won't be able to restore
everything

# Slide 4 - Backup strategy. Streaming backups


In some cases, having periodic backups doesn't make sense, for example if your data moves too fast
for a periodic backup. Or maybe you want to use your backup as a replica.

In this case, streaming backups, or replication may be a better option.

This is typically used in databases or other storage systems with high volume of data flowing.
And as the name suggests, in this case you only have the latest copy of your data that is constantly
being updated from you source dataset in realtime. You may still want to make periodic backups
for the cases of all the data being deleted somehow and deletes replicating to all the replicas.
This gives you the ability to use your backup as a readonly copy for you service improving read performance.
And in the cases when your source dataset becomes corrupted or unavailable, your backup can become the new source of
truth almost immediately (low downtime), this is called fail-over.

You may even want to have several read-replicas in different regions for both a more robust
backup solution and for faster access for all users across the globe

# Slide 5 - Hot and cold storage

Ok, you are done with choosing how you are going to back up your data.
Another important thing to consider is where this data is going to be stored.

There are 2 types of data storage: hot and cold.

Hot storage is when after writing the data, accessing and reading the data is just as fast
When you need frequent access to the data, this is what you use

Cold storage is the opposite of hot storage obviously, this is when the data you have written
can't be access directly of fast because it is moved to a slow storage device, disconnected
from the server and literally stored somewhere on the shelf.


-------------------------------------------------------------
# Slide 6 - Hot storage

Hot storage mediums are more expensive, faster and more dense than cold storage mediums
You can use ssds or hdds for your hot storage. With ssds being the fastest, very power efficient
but the more expensive option.
Hdds are slower, especially for random reads and writes, less power efficient, but cheaper per TB.

In a general purpose storage array hdds are used together with ssds, with the ssds serving as a read-write
cache for the system, effectively raising the performance to ssd-like speed while keeping the cost reasonably low.


-------------------------------------------------------------
# Slide 7 - Cold storage

Cold storage doesn't have to be fast or energy efficient. It just has to be durable for long-term
storage with the second consideration being density.

So for this LTO (magnetic) tape or optical discs.

Tape storage is slow to read and write, but is very durable while being relatively cheap
and offering densities comparable to enterprise hdds.
Shelf life for the magnetic tape is up to 30 years. The price per TB is about 3x less.
The largest tape I found on sale right now is 22TB and the largest hdd is 32TB

Optical storage is another option. Optical disks have an even longer shelf life
extending up to 150 years, but their density is not impressive at all.
You'll be paying 10x for the same amount of data, as you would if you bought tape.

You could of course store hard drives, but they are more fragile and have more moving parts
than tape or optical. While the data on the magnetic platers inside will stay there for quite a while,
the electronics and moving parts may stop working after a very long time of just laying.


-------------------------------------------------------------
# Slide 8 - Importance of testing your backups

Testing you backups is as important as having them in the first place.
If the backup can't be restored, then what's the point in having it in the first place.

So it's a good idea to regularly test recovery on a dummy system to make sure that you actually back up everything
that is required for the system to operate and that restoring it actually works anc can be done in the desired time-frame.
It's also a good idea to test the intergrity of your backups.

# Slide 9 - Backup compression

A note on compression.

When you back up your data, you may want to compress the backups. This trades cpu time
and RAM usage when backing up and restoring for storage the backup takes.

It's possible to compress the data itself inside the backup.
And it's possible to deduplicate files across backups as well on the filesystem level.
Compression ratio will largely depend on the type and nature of your data as well as on the
algorithm used.

For storing data on a cold storage device, a very high compression ratio might be set,
because all we care in that case is storing as much data as possible on the device and extra cpu time cost is acceptable.

# Slide 10 - Backup tools used in enterprise

The backups are usually managed by the service (software) operator, not the datacenter (hardware) operator
except maybe for some special cases. So when renting hardware in a datacenter, it's
your responsibility to back up your data. The datacenter operator only protects you from
hardware failure on their side using RAID and preemptive disk swapping.

Some of the tools you can use in a enterprise setting are:
- Native filesystem snapshots if you manage the filesystem and are running on ZFS of BTRFS,
This is closest to an incremental backup, but managed at the filesystem level.
- HYCU
- Rubrik
- Acronis
- etc.

I frankly haven't tried or seen any of those, at my workplace (Penki) we have python script that copies files
using rsync, and block-level nightly disk backups for some critical systems since we manage our own hardware.

# Slide 11 - Backup tools for you to use

Of course you as a consumer should back up your data as well to protect yourself from
losing your phone or computer.

On the modern phones some data is already backed up by default for you if you log into
your google or apple account. This includes your contacts, phone settings, installed applications,
sometimes application data as well.
You can also set up photo backups as well with the same provider with a few clicks if you don't
mind paying for a subscription that provides more cloud storage for your account.

On devices running windows, if you are logged into a microsoft account,
computer settings and wifi passwords are already backed up by default.

And it's possible to set up file backups using OneDrive. But I would
strongly recommend against doing that because microsoft's implementation is confusing
and subpar. They try to frame onedrive as an extension of your physical drive and
you may not know whether your files are in the cloud or physically on your disk or both when using a
onedrive synced folder leading to confusion and lost files.

Instead, for file backups I would recommend a standalone tool like
- FreeFileSync - Supports multiple cloud providers and is flexible with
what you want to back up, how often and when
- FolderSync - This is like the previous opition, but with a simpler UI and a little less flexible
- Syncthing - If you prefer to store data on your own devices, this may be an option for you,
this tool provides one-way or two-way peer-to-peer syncing between your devices.

These tools apply to you if you are running macos or linux as well, they are cross-platform.

FolderSync and Syncthing are available for android as well, so you can have the same tool
across all of your devices. And with syncthing you can have your files synced between your devices without
relying on a cloud or paying anyone.

On a mac, you can also use the icloud for backups and TimeMachine for periodic backups.

On an iphone you are more limited, you can't even browse the filesystem on there. But you can use
iCloud for backups or back up your phone from a PC using iTunes or iMazing.

On linux you can use whatewer you like, there are way too many options to list here,
but since 90% of the servers run linux, you can imagine there are a lot of options.
Filesystem snapshots I mentioned earlier, borg backup, rsync, rclone, etc.

# Slide 12 - A note about RAID

RAID (redundant array of inexpensive disks) is not a backup,
it only protects you from a physical failure of one or 2 drives in
a storage array, but does not protect you from any other unexpected events

While it is not a backup, it is still very useful for protecting against drive failure in
a storage array and should be used when dealing with large amounts of data across multiple drives.
